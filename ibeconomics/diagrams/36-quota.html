An import quota caps the number of units that can be imported. The domestic price rises to clear what remains. Visually similar to a tariff — the shapes on the diagram are the same — but the purple rectangle is quota rent and goes to whoever holds the import licences (often foreign exporters who raise their prices), not to the home government.

Quota vs tariff. A quota and a tariff can produce the same domestic price, quantities, and welfare losses. The difference is who gets the "middle rectangle". With a tariff, it's government tax revenue. With a quota, it's rent captured by whoever holds the import licences — often foreign exporters raising their own prices to capture the scarcity rent. That's one reason economists generally prefer tariffs to quotas: at least the revenue stays in the country.
