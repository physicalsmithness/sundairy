The money market: equilibrium interest rate

What happens
The money market determines the short-run nominal interest rate. Money supply (Ms) is vertical — set by the central bank. Money demand (Md) slopes down — at higher interest rates, the opportunity cost of holding money rather than bonds is higher, so people hold less cash.
How large is the central bank's operation or the shift in money demand?

What's happening

Baseline equilibrium
Interest rate i 5.0%
Money stock M 200

At the baseline equilibrium, Ms = Md at i = 5%. The central bank has fixed the money supply at 200. Any deviation from i = 5% creates excess demand or supply for money and pushes i back to equilibrium.

How monetary policy works. When the central bank wants to lower interest rates, it buys government bonds from the public, paying with newly created money — Ms shifts right, i falls. When it wants to raise rates, it sells bonds, sucking money out — Ms shifts left, i rises. The interest rate then transmits to borrowing costs across the economy, affecting investment, consumption, and (through exchange rates) net exports.
