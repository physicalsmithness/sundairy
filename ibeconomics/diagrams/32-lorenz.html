Lorenz curve & Gini coefficient

Country / example
The Lorenz curve plots cumulative share of income against cumulative share of population, ranked from poorest to richest. The 45° line represents perfect equality (bottom 10% earn 10% of income, etc). The further the actual Lorenz curve bows below, the more unequal the distribution.
Adjust the curvature. Higher values = greater inequality (the curve bows further below the 45° line).

The Gini coefficient summarises the whole Lorenz curve as a single number: G = A / (A+B), where A is the area between the 45° line and the Lorenz curve, and B is the area under the Lorenz curve. G = 0 means perfect equality; G = 1 means one person has all the income.

Reading the Gini. Nordic countries cluster around 0.25-0.30 (low inequality, high taxes and transfers). Most developed economies sit 0.30-0.45. Developing economies often 0.45-0.55. Some Latin American and African countries exceed 0.55. No country is below 0.22 or above about 0.65. Over time, the UK and US have seen rising inequality since 1980; most of continental Europe has been relatively stable.
