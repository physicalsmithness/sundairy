Lorenz curve and Gini coefficient

Real-world Gini
Slovakia has one of the most equal income distributions in the world (Gini ≈ 25). The Lorenz curve sits close to the line of equality — each slice of the population earns a similar share of total income.
Higher values = more unequal. 0 is perfect equality; 3+ is extreme concentration. Real-world countries sit between 0.5 and 2.5.

The Lorenz curve plots the cumulative share of income (y) against the cumulative share of the population (x), both from poorest to richest. Perfect equality would be a straight 45° line — bottom 10% earn 10% of income, bottom half earn half. Any actual economy bows below this line; the bigger the bow, the worse the inequality.

Gini = A / (A + B). A is the area between the line of equality and the Lorenz curve (coral shading); B is the area below the Lorenz curve (green). If the Lorenz curve coincides with the equality line, A=0 → Gini=0 (perfect equality). If it hugs the x-axis until the last person and then spikes to 100%, A fills the whole triangle → Gini=1 (one person has everything). Real Gini values are reported as either 0-1 or 0-100.
