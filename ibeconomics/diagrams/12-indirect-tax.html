Indirect tax

Real-world case
An indirect tax is a tax on a transaction, paid by firms but often passed on (in part) to consumers. Slide the tax rate and watch the wedge open between what buyers pay and what sellers keep.

An indirect tax creates a wedge between the price consumers pay and the price producers receive. The government collects tax × quantity traded. Fewer units are traded overall, and the surplus that disappears is the deadweight loss.

Tax incidence. The side of the market that is more inelastic bears more of the tax. For a good with inelastic demand (cigarettes, petrol), consumers can't easily cut back, so they shoulder most of the tax. For a good with inelastic supply (e.g. land), producers shoulder it. This is why governments often tax inelastic goods: it raises revenue without much quantity distortion.
