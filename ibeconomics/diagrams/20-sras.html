Short-run aggregate supply

Cause a shift
Short-run aggregate supply shows total output firms are willing to produce at each price level, holding production costs fixed. It slopes upward because higher prices mean higher profits (given sticky wages) and firms respond by producing more. Pick a cause to see SRAS shift.

No shift

Anything that changes the cost of producing output shifts SRAS. Higher input costs, wages, or production taxes shift SRAS left. Higher productivity or subsidies shift SRAS right. In the short run, wages are "sticky" — slow to respond to changes in the price level — which is why SRAS slopes up rather than being vertical.

Why SRAS slopes up. In the short run, nominal wages are fixed by existing contracts. If the price level rises, the real wage firms pay falls, making labour effectively cheaper, so firms produce more. As output approaches full-capacity (full employment), the curve gets steeper because extra output requires more overtime, temporary hires, and other costly adjustments.
