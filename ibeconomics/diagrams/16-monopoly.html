Monopoly

A monopolist faces the entire market demand curve. Because D slopes down, selling one more unit requires cutting price on all units, so MR < price. Profit-max where MR = MC, then charges the price D allows at that quantity.

The monopolist picks q where MR = MC, then charges the highest price consumers will pay for that quantity (on the demand curve). Because price exceeds MC at the chosen q, allocative efficiency is lost — some consumers would pay more than the marginal cost of an extra unit but don't get one.

Key idea. Unlike perfect competition, a monopoly can sustain economic profit in the long run because barriers to entry (patents, network effects, economies of scale, legal protection) prevent competitors from bidding the price down. The welfare cost shows up as both higher prices and lost trades.
