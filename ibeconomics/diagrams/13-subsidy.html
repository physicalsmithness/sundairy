A subsidy opens a wedge between the price consumers pay and the price producers receive, but in the opposite direction from a tax: consumers pay less, producers get more, and the government pays the difference per unit. More units are traded, but the cost to government exceeds the gain in consumer and producer surplus — the difference is deadweight loss.

Who benefits. The side of the market that is more inelastic captures more of the subsidy — symmetric to tax incidence. Subsidies are often used when a good has positive externalities (solar, education, vaccines) so the "deadweight loss" from an equilibrium perspective is actually a welfare gain once the externality benefit is counted.
