Keynesian vs neoclassical AS

View of AS
The Keynesian school argues that in the short run, wages and prices are sticky downward, so the economy can operate below full employment for long periods with unused capacity. AS is flat until Yf.
Shift AD left (recession / austerity) or right (boom / stimulus). Same AD shift produces different outcomes depending on where you are on AS — that's the whole point.
Tint the three sections of AS (Keynesian / intermediate / classical) so you can see which section the equilibrium is in.

The Keynesian AS curve is flat up to Yf and then vertical. Below Yf, AD shifts produce changes in output with no inflation (the flat portion). At Yf, any further AD increase produces pure inflation.

Why this disagreement matters. If you're a Keynesian, fiscal or monetary stimulus below Yf produces free output — no inflation cost. If you're neoclassical, the same stimulus just causes inflation because output is fixed at Yf by real factors. This is the core macroeconomic dispute of the 20th century — and it's still live.
