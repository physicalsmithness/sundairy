Crowding-out effect

The government expands fiscal policy (G↑ or T↓), but it has to borrow to finance this. The borrowing raises interest rates, which reduces private investment. The net effect on AD is smaller than the "intended" increase: this is crowding-out.

With moderate fiscal expansion and moderate investment sensitivity, crowding-out reduces the effective AD shift by around a third. Fiscal policy still works, but less than its face value suggests.

When crowding-out is weak or strong. Crowding-out is weak when the economy is in a liquidity trap (interest rates near zero and can't rise much), when investment isn't very interest-sensitive (firms invest based on demand expectations more than rates), or when the economy has lots of slack. It's strong when the economy is near full capacity, interest rates have room to rise, and firms are highly sensitive to borrowing costs. This is why Keynesians and new-classical economists often disagree about the effectiveness of fiscal policy.
