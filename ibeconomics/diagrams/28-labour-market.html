The labour market works like any other market — wage W is the price of an hour of labour, and quantity is hours worked (or jobs). D_L slopes down: firms hire less labour at higher wages. S_L slopes up: more workers want to work at higher wages. At W*, the market clears.

Minimum wage trade-off. A binding minimum wage raises wages for those who keep their jobs (a distributional win) but creates unemployment among those who would have been hired at the lower wage (an efficiency loss). How big the unemployment effect is depends on how elastic labour demand is. Empirical studies in the UK and US have found surprisingly small effects — labour demand appears to be quite inelastic at current minimum-wage levels.
