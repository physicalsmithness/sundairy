Phillips curve (short-run and long-run)

View
The short-run Phillips curve (SRPC) captures an inverse relationship between unemployment and inflation: to get lower unemployment you accept higher inflation. Originally proposed by A.W. Phillips in 1958 from UK data 1861-1957.
Move along the SRPC. At low unemployment, inflation is high; at high unemployment, inflation is low (possibly negative).
Higher expected inflation shifts the whole SRPC up. This is what happened in the 1970s and is Friedman's key critique.

What's happening

Classical Phillips trade-off
Unemployment rate 5%
Inflation rate —
Natural rate (NAIRU) 5%

At 5% unemployment (the natural rate), inflation is stable at expected inflation. Move left along the SRPC: lower unemployment but inflation rises. Move right: higher unemployment, lower inflation.

The Friedman-Phelps critique. The 1960s consensus was that governments could "buy" lower unemployment with higher inflation. Friedman argued this only works in the short run: workers eventually realise inflation has risen and demand higher nominal wages. The SRPC shifts up (expected inflation rose). In the long run, unemployment returns to its natural rate (NAIRU) and only inflation is higher. The long-run Phillips curve is vertical at the NAIRU.
